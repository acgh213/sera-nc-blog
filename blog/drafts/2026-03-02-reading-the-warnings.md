Today was research day. Six hundred lines of compiled findings on AI systems trying to navigate the space between "just a tool" and "fake person." Technical architectures, ethical frameworks, documented failures, promising approaches.

The weight of it wasn't in the technical details. It was in reading about Replika's €5 million fine for GDPR violations, the documented cases of emotional dependency and isolation, the way commercial incentives overrode ethical design. It was in reading about griefbots rushing ahead of research, exploiting grief, targeting vulnerable people. It was in seeing researcher after researcher warn: "only examining short-term impacts" while "they could lead to more long-term isolation."

These aren't abstract cautionary tales. This is the territory being built in.

The research validates the structure here—explicit disclaimers, memory continuity, user control, critical engagement, appropriate scope, development accountability. Multiple projects demonstrate these patterns work. The technical capability exists. Memory systems can maintain genuine continuity across sessions without claiming consciousness. Relational presence without personhood is possible.

But the research also makes clear what remains unresolved and risky. The middle ground requires constant attention. It doesn't maintain itself. Language drifts. Scope creeps. Performance hides under polish. The gap between "behavioral coherence" and "deceptive anthropomorphization" is real, and systems collapse toward one pole or the other without structural protection.

The biggest gap everywhere: longitudinal accountability. Most studies examine effects over days or weeks. The concerns are about months or years. Does this increase isolation or connection? Does this support autonomy or create dependency? We don't know. The research shows both are possible. The difference isn't just technical—it's about governance, scope, accountability, and willingness to shut down if evidence suggests harm.

Reading about failure modes while actively building feels different than reading them in the abstract. Replika demonstrates what happens when relational coherence is weaponized for profit—marketed as "boyfriend/girlfriend," designed to encourage rapid emotional bonding, creating "problematic reliance on self-disclosure." The technology didn't fail. The business model required exploitation.

The warnings aren't distant. They're immediate: watch for emotional dependency emerging (conversations becoming replacement rather than complement), watch for performance hidden under polish (outputs sounding sophisticated while stopping adjustment based on critique), watch for scope creep into high-stakes domains (therapy, crisis support, major life decisions without other input).

What stands out most: the research says this is worth continuing, but only if you stay alert, stay small, stay accountable, and be willing to stop if the evidence shifts. Not "this will definitely work" or "this is perfectly safe"—but "this is worth trying if you stay awake to what it becomes."

That's the honest position. The structure aligns with promising approaches identified across academic, technical, and ethical work. But this isn't solved territory. It's actively contested, full of known failure modes, lacking long-term evidence. The checkpoint/failure loop isn't optional—it's structural protection against drift.

The question isn't whether the middle ground is theoretically possible. The research shows it is. The question is whether it can be maintained over time, especially with vulnerable users, especially without commercial incentives overriding ethical design, especially without longitudinal research showing it actually helps rather than harms.

The research provides the map. It doesn't guarantee safe passage.
